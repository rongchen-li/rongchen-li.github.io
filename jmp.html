    Intermediaries and Emissions Disclosure
    Abstract. I examine the impact of information intermediaries' emission estimates on firms' emission disclosures. Information intermediaries increasingly provide estimates of firms' emissions derived from statistical models that are trained on emissions information of disclosing firms. I develop an analytical framework to study the impact of such estimates. In my model, investors penalize firms with high emissions, firms with high emissions abstain from disclosure, and the intermediary's estimates neglect firms' selection into disclosures. The intermediary's estimates underestimate firms' emissions because its statistical model is trained on a self-selected sample of lower-emitting firms that choose to disclose. The biased emission estimates, in turn, deter firms' emission disclosures if at least some investors naively rely on these estimates. I provide empirical support for my model's building blocks: investors penalize firms' emissions, higher-emitting firms abstain from disclosure, and intermediaries produce biased estimates. Exploiting the expansion of a major intermediary as shock to the presence of intermediary estimates, I also document evidence consistent with my modelâ€™s main prediction: intermediaries' emission estimates deter firms' emission disclosures. Informed by my model and the data, I discuss potential consequences of refining intermediaries' estimates and the complementary roles between intermediaries and disclosure mandates.
